Current Focus: Mapping Albedo Zones of New Lunar Impacts

Main Goal:

To systematically map and characterize the extensive bright and dark zones (albedo features) surrounding the newest impact craters on the Moon and determine what causes them.

The Mystery of the Extensive Ejecta

Recent work (Speyerer et al., 2016) has identified over 200 new impact craters on the Moon created in the last decade. What's striking is that these new impacts are often surrounded by extensive radial albedo features that can stretch for tens of kilometersâ€”up to 50 times the crater radius.

My research aims to investigate how the scale of these features correlates with latitude, the dustiness and geological settings of the terrain, and their similarity to known lunar cold spots.

Cold spots are thermally distinct areas (colder than their surroundings) observed by the LRO Diviner instrument. Cold spots are thought to be caused by low-density, fluffy material that hasn't yet compacted through space weathering.

An example of a cold spot crater shown in Diviner H-Parameter data [9] where dark blue is low thermal inertia and light blue is higher (M1224603815RC). The colormap in A is inverted.

Pipeline

To address this, I've developed a Python pipeline to process high-resolution imagery from the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter (LRO) Narrow Angle Camera (NAC):

  1. The pipeline automatically queries the LRO image database to find optimal before-and-after image pairs for 77 new impact sites.
  2. The pipeline georeferences and tone-matches these images, enhancing them to isolate and highlight the subtle albedo changes caused by the fresh impact ejecta.
    3. Georeferenced and tone-matched set of images before and after the new impact event.
  3. It isolates the albedo change by dividing the before-and-after image pair.
    4. Resulting image after dividing the set of georeferenced and tone-matched before-and-after images. This isolates the change caused by the impact.
  4. Using arcGIS and QGIS (GIS software), I then measure crater size and precisely map the extent and asymmetry of the ejecta zones.
    5. Ejecta mapping (M1237422334RC). In the top left, the location of a radial transect (red) is shown. In the top right the continuous and discontinuous ejecta layers are mapped. Bottom shows pixel intensity analysis. Locations of B-D features are highlighted in blue.
